General Patient Discharge Instructions

This document provides essential guidelines for your recovery following discharge from the hospital. Please read these instructions carefully and share them with any caregivers assisting you at home. Your health and safety are our highest priorities during this transition period.

Activity Restrictions
For the first two weeks following your discharge, strict activity restrictions apply. You must avoid lifting any object heavier than ten pounds, which includes groceries, pets, and small children. Strenuous physical activities such as running, swimming, or aerobic exercises are strictly prohibited until cleared by your physician. You may engage in light walking around your home to promote healthy circulation, but you should stop immediately if you experience shortness of breath, dizziness, or severe pain. Driving a motor vehicle is not permitted while you are taking prescription opioid pain medications or if your physical mobility is significantly impaired. Please arrange for a friend or family member to provide transportation during this time.

Wound Care Guidelines
If you have a surgical incision or wound, proper care is critical to prevent infection. Keep the dressing clean and dry at all times. You may gently wash the area around the wound with mild soap and water after 48 hours, but do not scrub the incision directly or submerge the wound in a bathtub, swimming pool, or hot tub. Pat the area dry with a clean towel; do not rub. Inspect the wound daily for any signs of infection.

Medication Instructions
Resume taking your daily medications only as directed by your discharging physician. Do not take any over-the-counter medications, vitamins, or herbal supplements without consulting your doctor first, as they may interact with your new prescriptions. Take all prescribed antibiotics exactly as directed until the entire course is finished, even if you begin to feel better before the medication is gone. Pain medication should be taken exactly as prescribed, ideally with food to minimize stomach upset.

Warning Signs to Watch For
You must monitor your condition closely for any potential complications. If you experience any of the following warning signs, seek medical attention immediately. These include a persistent fever of 101 degrees Fahrenheit (38.3 degrees Celsius) or higher, sudden or worsening shortness of breath, a sudden onset of severe chest pain, coughing up blood, or any new or worsening confusion or altered mental state.

When to Call the Doctor vs. Go to the Emergency Room
It is vital to know when to contact your primary care physician versus when to seek emergency care. You should call your doctor's office during normal business hours for non-urgent matters, such as minor nausea, constipation, questions about your medication schedule, or a low-grade fever below 101 degrees Fahrenheit. However, you must go to the nearest Emergency Room or dial 911 immediately if you experience life-threatening symptoms, including sudden chest pain, difficulty breathing, sudden severe bleeding that does not stop with direct pressure, drooping on one side of your face, or sudden difficulty speaking.

Follow-Up Appointment Requirement
A mandatory follow-up appointment with your primary care provider or specialist must be scheduled and attended within 7 days of your discharge date. This appointment is crucial for evaluating your recovery progress, reviewing your current medications, and assessing the healing of any wounds. Please call the clinic within 24 hours of returning home to schedule this visit if it was not already arranged prior to your departure from the hospital. Failure to attend this follow-up appointment may result in delayed recovery or unrecognized complications.
